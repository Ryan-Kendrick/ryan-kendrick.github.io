Ryan's Thoughts

Strengths, Values, Limitations

When a person is asked about their core values they will often cite mentors that influenced them or positive experiences in which they were inspired in such a way that it permanently altered their character. I cannot define myself that way. I always come up blank when I try to approach the question from a positive angle. I seldom see a person try to define themselves by recounting experiences of discordance and how it defined their relation to The Other. Maybe it's not socially acceptable to define oneself in negative terms, but I feel it's a far more honest approach, at least in some cases.

Formative Experiences

The first time I seriously considered the validity of defining myself in negative terms was at a conference at Victoria University. We were to discuss our values, defining experiences, and cultural influences with a partner who was whoever was sitting next to us. My partner gave a typical response involving her grandparents inspiring her to honesty, integrity, and the value of Tikanga Māori. I, as usual, came up blank. However, my partner pointed out to me that actually my character had been significantly shaped by my mean grandmother and unscrupulous parents, just not in the same positive manner that she had been influenced. The hypocrisy of my mother in preaching honesty and fairness while lying and taking any advantage she could get at any price had, in the dissonance it stirred up in me, inspired me. Likewise, the rugged individualism and the tough love approach of my father helped me clearly define what the Other is, and in defining the Other, I was defining myself.

As a result of the incompatibility between my gentler, more conscientious personality and the personality and interaction styles of those around me at various points in my upbringing, I developed a strong appreciation for honesty, integrity, kindness, and a sense of justice. The dispassion expressed in the classic utilitarian maxim of “the greatest good for the greatest number” has guided all of my most difficult ethical decisions.

Ethics

One time at work I noticed that a supplier of chilled food products would quickly swap out meals that failed a temperature check. We had few formal policies and usually allowed suppliers to swap out goods that failed a temperature check until we found a single unit that had an acceptable temperature. Once we found a single unit within the acceptable temperature range, the rest of the order went unchecked. Temperature checks, like the rest of health and safety, was merely a formality and nobody really cared except for the head of health and safety. I generally went along with it even though it was a dodgy practice, because when you work at a supermarket the corruption and bad-faith practices are endemic, and unless the situation is extremely egregious there is no point in trying to raise an issue. No one cares.

This supplier's products were in the 30C to 40C range - the “danger zone” where bacteria rapidly reproduces - and I told my coworker that perhaps this is so bad that we should report it to health and safety. Unexpectedly, my coworker begged me not to, given how stretched thin we were in the storeroom. If the resolution were that we had to call health and safety or go through every single unit every time this order arrived it would make our already difficult jobs even more difficult. Reporting the incident to health and safety was a clear betrayal of the storeroom and my fellow workers. Ultimately, I decided to report the incident. The additional hardship that may result from reporting the incident to health and safety I judged to be lesser than the health risk to the public if this supplier were able to continue to deliver meals that arrive at unsafe temperatures. “The greatest good for the greatest number”.

Upon reflection, I am satisfied with my decision to betray my coworkers. The burden of thoroughly checking this supplier's products ended up being put on another department that was more able to shoulder that burden, and even if it wasn't, I still think the risk to the public was too great not to take action.

Strengths and Weaknesses

My strengths of patience, drive, and focus will keep me on track through all of the struggle that this course intentionally provokes. My strong sense of justice and integrity will ensure it is done right and I am able to productively work with others. I will need to keep a check on apprehension and self-consciousness to make sure it doesn't hold me back. I will need to interrogate negative thoughts to figure out if they have a rational basis and highlight something I need to address, or if they are just echoes of something in the past that has no bearing on the present situation. I certainly have a history of not putting myself out there enough and making excuses to avoid challenging situations that could in fact lead to growth.

Conflict With Others

We had a new manager helping out in the storeroom on a particularly busy day when my boss called in sick. This manager had a reputation for asking few questions before making demands. During the day, it is imperative that I work in the main storeroom whenever there are orders to be processed. The manager in question was furious when I refused to help him with heavy lifting in another room because the storeroom was depending on me to make space by working in the main storeroom. I explained to this manager how the storeroom generally operates under my boss and the fact that a coworker needed me to continue the task I was working on in order for the storeroom to operate smoothly. The manager insinuated I was simply being lazy and stormed off to report me to the store manager. As the store manager didn’t respect this manager, the incident did not go any further.

My strategy of explaining what I was doing and why was ineffective in resolving the conflict and led to the manager refusing to assist the storeroom. It did not address the feeling of envy he felt that he had to do more difficult manual labour than I was doing at that time. It would have been more effective to empathise by speaking to his feelings first before getting to the facts, but because I had come in to work early specifically to help my colleague I felt angry at the fact this manager would come into the storeroom and try to help us by making poorly informed demands. Although I don't necessarily regret my poor handling of the conflict given the toxic environment I was in, now that I’m no longer in such an environment I hope to handle such incidents with a lot more empathy and speak to feelings before speaking to facts.
