Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

The principles of neuroplasticity in the context of learning highlight the importance of practise and perseverance. Neuroplasticity teaches us that our brains are always fine-tuning by eliminating underutilised pathways and strengthening new ones - especially when we're engaging in a task in which we're encountering resistance.

It is much like strength training in the gym. Our muscles grow when an adaptive response is triggered by repeat exposure to stress. Our bodies send and receive the signal that the current configuration of our bodies is not up to the task. In response, our bodies make the necessary changes to allow increased muscle mass or brain connectivity in certain areas to allow us to better meet the demands of the challenge.

Understanding these principles can help learners to understand the importance of not giving up when acquiring a new skill. Resistance, struggle, and failure are all part of the process of learning. Becoming successful and gaining new abilities means stomaching the uncomfortable feelings that come up when you do encounter difficulties. Feeling incompetent and like you are struggling is necessary because, well, you are. We all are at some point, and the only way anyone ever becomes competent is by practising as an incompetent person until they become competent.

It is possible to speed up the process. Exercising your body and exercising your brain can create an environment more conducive to neuroplasticity and learning. Tools like analogies to link new ideas to ones you already understand can make it easier to remember new ideas. It is easier to build on existing concepts than create new ones. Additionally, it is helpful to take theory and immediately see it in practice. In my case where I am learning to code, this means taking concepts from lectures, articles and technical documentation and applying them to real projects where I can learn to use them and see how they work in practice.

We tend to learn from experience and then generalise from that experience to form hypotheses or beliefs about ourselves. For me, I learned at an early age from the feedback I was given that I fit into a kind of intelligent but shy and socially inept archetype of a person. Teaching people, explaining things, leadership, and being an inspiration for others just wasn't in my essence. In recent years, being put in positions where I have had to learn to teach, lead, and inspire others has shattered my self-image and allowed me to grow beyond what I was once thought was possible. The feedback loop was finally broken.

Fixed and Growth Mindsets

A potential barrier to learning comes from this fixed mindset. In a fixed mindset, abilities are viewed as coming from character traits which are innate and unchanging. Struggle is viewed not as a sign of an engaged learner, but as evidence of a lack of natural aptitude. In other words, if there is resistance it shows you are not the right person for the job and it would perhaps be best for you to give up and leave it to someone that has the natural aptitude. I have been surprised in my research by the overlap between a fixed mindset and the fundamental attribution error. There is a general tendency toward viewing the actions and abilities of others as explained by fixed character traits rather than circumstances. A fixed mindset is like the fundamental attribution error turned inward.

In contrast, a growth mindset embraces our neuroplasticity and changes our relationship to criticism. It is the opposite of a fixed mindset. Rather than being a source of shame or an expression of hostility, criticism is an opportunity to learn, to fast-track our growth. In a growth mindset, struggle becomes not the essential ingredient not of failure, but of success.

Takeaway

For me, having a growth mindset in my learning means when I encounter situations that challenge me to lead, I recognise the opportunity to improve. For if I lack the ability to be a good leader, that means not that it is futile to try to lead, but instead, that if I do try to lead, growth will come easy, because struggle will come easy.
