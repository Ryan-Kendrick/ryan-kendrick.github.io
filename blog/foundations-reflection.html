Ryan's Thoughts

Reflections on Foundations

In this article I'm going to interview myself! Let's get started.

What are three new things you have learnt about yourself and your ego due to the core learning?

I learned more about the limits of my confidence in regards to learning. I used to think I was unflappable due to a lot of experiences that have reinforced that I am capable when it comes to learning new things, but just as quickly as new things will clicks there will be a new concept which requires understanding five other new concepts in order to be understood. Jumping into more familiar waters - doing a Scrimba rather than looking at classes and the prototype chain - helps to heal my bruised ego. I also learned that a growth mindet is what I have been trying to cultivate.

What are the role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning and programming?

Values and self-awareness inform our attitude towards ourselves, our learning, and struggles. They determine whether, upon getting stuck, we see a puzzle to solve that promotes growth, or we see proof of our incapability and either lash out or give up. Empathy is important for recognising the same struggles and attitudes in others. As is self-awareness. Just as we can encounter a challenge and in a moment of weakness take a self-destructive attitude, we can take that same attitude towards other people if we fail to put ourselves in their shoes.

What has surprised you the most about the core learning?

The way the neuroplasticity and fixed vs. growth mindset material formalised a lot of things I think about on and off. I have encountered a lot of people with very fixed mindets, who upon encountering a block will lash out at others or themselves in quite dramatic fashion. They will talk about how stupid either they or the source of the block is and it will ruin their day. These examples are always in the back of my mind when I encounter a block and are why I tend to persevere, even when I should probably take a break.

What were the most challenging aspects of the core learning?

The identity-values and learning-plan documents were the most challenging. There are always a lot of factors that go into making a person how they are, and so deconstructing that is always a lot of work. It also means digging up a lot of negativity since negative experiences seem to be a lot more sticky than positive ones.

Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on core learning in a web development Bootcamp course?

The crude answer is because IT people are notoriously bad at being people. They don't know how to work with others, can't empathise, can't communicate, have no self-awareness and no worldly experience.

The diplomatic answer is because we're here to gain employable skills and employers care more about our ability to work with others and contribute to their culture than our technical skills. We would be working in teams on projects and the empathetic, respectful communication that facilitates working towards a common goal is impossible without developing self-awareness.

Does the time you spent studying core learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practise programming instead? Justify your answer.

Kind of. The problem as I see it with core learning is that the skills being taught are not as unique as programming skills. You have to go out of your way to learn to programme. You don't have to go out of your way to have experiences which prompt you to consider your motivations and what makes you different to another person. It' more necessary to attend a programming school to productively learn to code than it is necessary to attend a programming school to learn to be kind, to reflect on your values.

With that said, we can always be better, and for myself I'm particularly interested in the actual practice of human skills come bootcamp. The skills I need to develop and confidence I need to build can only come from experience, so I look forward to moving beyond theory and reading articles.
